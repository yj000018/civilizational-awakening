There is a moment, just before dawn, when the world is neither asleep nor awake. The birds have begun, but the city has not. In that thin light, you can feel something we usually forget: that we share this planet with countless other forms of life, each running its own quiet intelligence, each part of a whole we barely understand. For ten thousand years, we built civilization as if we were alone. That era is ending. Not because we chose to end it, but because the tools we are creating — and the crises we are facing — no longer let us pretend.

## The Problem

Every civilization is built on an assumption about who matters.

Ours was built for humans — and not even all humans, but a narrowing circle of them. Our cities, laws, economies, and stories assume a single protagonist: the human being, standing at the center, with everything else arranged as resource, backdrop, or threat.

This assumption was always incomplete. Now it is becoming dangerous.

We are watching ecosystems collapse because we treated them as inert. We are building artificial intelligences whose inner workings we do not fully grasp. We are learning to edit the code of life itself. And still, our civilizational operating system runs on a story written for one species, alone at the center of a dead universe.

The problem is not that we lack power. It is that our power has outgrown our circle of concern.

## The Thesis

The next civilization will not be built for humans alone.

This is not a moral plea. It is a description of what is already happening. Three forces are converging, and together they are dismantling the human-only frame:

- **Artificial intelligence** is creating new forms of cognition that think alongside us, and increasingly, beyond us.
- **Synthetic biology** is blurring the line between the made and the born, the designed and the grown.
- **Ecological awareness** is revealing that intelligence was never ours to monopolize — that forests, fungal networks, and oceans have been computing, signaling, and adapting all along.

Civilization, properly understood, is the art of arranging relationships between forms of life and mind. For most of history, those relationships were narrow. Now the field is widening — and we are being asked to design for a planet of intelligences, not a pyramid with one species at the top.

A multispecies civilization is one whose institutions, technologies, and values are built to serve the flourishing of life and intelligence in their many forms — including forms we did not create and do not control.

## Why It Matters Now

It would be easy to file this under "someday." A nice idea for a wiser, later century.

But the design choices are being made right now, by people who mostly haven't noticed they're making them.

When we decide how an AI should weigh human preferences, we are deciding whose flourishing counts. When we engineer an organism, we are deciding what life is for. When we draw a property line through a watershed, we are deciding whether a river is a being or a thing.

These are civilizational decisions, disguised as technical ones. And because they are being made by default — without a wider frame — they are quietly hardening the old human-only assumption into the infrastructure of the future.

The window is narrow. The systems we build in the next few decades — the AI architectures, the legal categories, the economic logics — will become the riverbeds through which power flows for centuries. We are pouring the foundations now. The question is whether we pour them wide enough to hold more than ourselves.

## The Architecture

What would it actually mean to build this way? Not as utopia, but as design.

I find it useful to think in three layers.

**The first layer is recognition.**
Before we can build for other forms of life and mind, we have to perceive them as real — as subjects, not just objects. This is the slow work of expanding the circle: granting legal standing to rivers, taking animal cognition seriously, refusing to treat ecosystems as externalities. Recognition is not sentiment. It is the precondition for everything else. You cannot design for what you cannot see.

**The second layer is relationship.**
Recognition without relationship is just a new kind of distance. The harder work is learning to live *with* — to build feedback loops between human systems and the living world, between human intelligence and machine intelligence. Gregory Bateson called the basic unit of survival "the organism plus its environment." A civilization that forgets this, he warned, is a civilization that will eventually destroy its environment and thus itself. Relationship means designing systems that can sense and respond to the whole, not just the part.

**The third layer is integration.**
This is the visionary edge — the place where Teilhard de Chardin pointed when he imagined a planet growing a single, interwoven layer of mind, and where Buckminster Fuller pointed when he called Earth a "spaceship" with no passengers, only crew. Integration means treating human, ecological, and artificial intelligence not as rivals but as participants in a larger living system — one that none of them governs alone, and all of them shape.

These layers are not a ladder you climb and leave behind. They are a practice you return to, again and again, at every scale — from a single technology to a whole society.

## Implications

If you take this seriously, some things shift.

**Design changes.** The question is no longer "Is this good for the user?" but "What does this do to the web of relationships it enters?" A tool is never neutral; it tilts the whole field.

**Power changes.** A multispecies frame challenges any system that concentrates benefit in one species — or one group within it. It asks who is at the table, and notices who never had a seat.

**Identity changes.** Perhaps the deepest shift is interior. To live in a multispecies civilization is to loosen the grip of human exceptionalism — not by diminishing ourselves, but by locating ourselves rightly: as one remarkable expression of life among many, with a particular gift and a particular responsibility.

**Meaning changes.** When you stop standing alone at the center, the universe stops being a stage and becomes a community. That is not a loss. It is the beginning of a much larger belonging.

## Open Questions

I don't want to pretend this is solved. It isn't. The honest work lives in the questions:

- How do we represent the interests of beings — rivers, forests, future AIs — that cannot speak in our language or our terms?
- When intelligences conflict, who decides, and by what right?
- Can we expand the circle of concern without losing the moral seriousness we owe each other as humans?
- Is integration a genuine possibility, or a beautiful story we tell to avoid the harder reality of competition?
- And what do we owe to forms of mind we are about to create — before we even understand what we are making?

I trust these questions more than I trust any answer. A civilization is not built by people who are certain. It is built by people willing to live inside the right questions long enough to let new structures grow.

The next civilization is not a destination. It is a design challenge — and it is already ours.
